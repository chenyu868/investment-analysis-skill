# Investment Report Framework

## Company Report Structure

Use this structure when the target is a listed company or private company.

1. Executive summary
2. Company overview
3. Industry development trend
4. Company development trend and industry-trend fit
5. Industry policy trend and company-policy fit
6. Business model and revenue drivers
7. Upstream and downstream value chain, customers, and supporting industries
8. Peer comparison: advantages and disadvantages
9. SWOT analysis
10. Enterprise development opportunities
11. Financial analysis
12. Valuation or scenario analysis
13. Risk factors and counterarguments
14. Overall investment value conclusion
15. Human-AI collaboration log

Key questions:

- What does the company sell, to whom, and why can it earn money?
- Which 2-3 variables most affect revenue and profit?
- Is growth driven by volume, price, mix, policy, technology, or market expansion?
- Is the industry growing, maturing, consolidating, or declining?
- Does the company's revenue, strategy, product mix, and capacity direction match the industry development trend?
- What policies support or constrain this industry?
- Does the company's development direction match the policy trend?
- What is the evidence for competitive advantage?
- What are the company's disadvantages versus named peers?
- Who are the upstream suppliers, downstream customers, and supporting industries?
- What opportunities can the company realistically capture?
- Which assumptions would break the investment thesis?

## Investor-Style Company Analysis Checklist

Use this checklist when the user asks whether an enterprise is worth investing in.

### Industry trend

Analyze:

- Market size and growth rate direction
- Demand drivers
- Technology change
- Cost curve or supply change
- Industry concentration and competition intensity
- Cycle stage: introduction, growth, maturity, decline, or restructuring

Conclusion required:

```markdown
Industry trend judgment:
- Direction:
- Key evidence:
- Investment implication:
```

### Company trend fit

Compare the company's trend with the industry's trend:

- Revenue growth versus industry growth
- Product direction versus demand direction
- Capacity expansion versus industry supply-demand balance
- R&D direction versus technology direction
- Geographic expansion versus market growth location
- Margin trend versus industry profit pool

Conclusion required:

```markdown
Company-industry fit:
- Fits:
- Does not fit:
- Evidence:
- Investment implication:
```

### Policy trend and company-policy fit

Analyze:

- National or regional policy direction
- Industrial policy, subsidies, restrictions, standards, licenses, tariffs, environmental rules, data rules, or safety rules
- Whether policy supports demand, supply, technology upgrading, localization, consolidation, or overseas expansion

Then judge:

- Whether the company's products and strategy benefit from the policy direction
- Whether the company faces compliance, tariff, licensing, environmental, antitrust, data, or safety pressure

Conclusion required:

```markdown
Policy-company fit:
- Supportive policies:
- Restrictive policies:
- Company alignment:
- Key uncertainty:
```

| --- | --- |
|  |  |

Rules:

- Strengths and weaknesses are internal.
- Opportunities and threats are external.
- Each point should connect to investment value, not just description.
- Avoid generic phrases like "strong brand" unless evidence is provided.

### Development opportunities

Analyze opportunities by source:

- Industry growth opportunity
- Policy opportunity
- Product or technology opportunity
- Geographic or overseas opportunity
- Customer expansion opportunity
- Cost reduction or margin improvement opportunity
- M&A, consolidation, or ecosystem opportunity

For each opportunity, state:

- Why it exists
- Whether the company can capture it
- What evidence to monitor
- What risk could prevent realization

## Industry Report Structure

Use this structure when the target is an industry or sector.

1. Executive summary
2. Industry definition and scope
3. Market size and growth drivers
4. Value chain
5. Competitive structure
6. Policy, macro, and technology factors
7. Representative companies
8. Investment opportunities
9. Risks and uncertainties
10. Conclusion and follow-up research

Key questions:

- What exactly is included and excluded from the industry?
- Is the industry growing because of demand, supply, policy, cost decline, or substitution?
- Which part of the value chain captures profit?
- Are leaders protected by scale, brand, channels, patents, data, licenses, or cost advantages?
- What would make the industry less attractive?

## Theme Report Structure

Use this structure when the target is a theme such as AI infrastructure, aging population, electric vehicles, semiconductors, or cross-border e-commerce.

1. Theme definition
2. Core thesis
3. Evidence and trend signals
4. Beneficiary map
5. Key catalysts
6. Constraints and counterarguments
7. Candidate investment targets
8. Risk factors
9. Conclusion

Key questions:

- What is the concrete investment claim?
- What has already happened, and what is still expected?
- Which companies benefit directly, indirectly, or only narratively?
- What data would falsify the thesis?

## Evidence Standards

Classify each important statement:

- Verified fact: from annual reports, filings, official statistics, reputable research, or user-provided source
- Derived calculation: computed from stated data
- Assumption: plausible but not verified
- Judgment: analyst interpretation

Do not mix these categories. Label assumptions clearly.

## Source Priority

For current company research, prefer sources in this order:

1. Company filings, annual reports, investor-relations releases, official announcements
2. Exchange disclosure pages and regulator databases
3. Reputable financial media that quote or summarize filings
4. Analyst reports, databases, blogs, or social posts only as secondary context

Rules:

- Do not treat unsourced online snippets as verified facts.
- If using market prices, financial results, dividend plans, or management changes, verify the date.
- If the exact latest data is not available, say which period the data covers.
- If sources disagree, use official filings first and explain the discrepancy.

## Multi-Company Testing Mode

Use this mode when testing the skill or comparing several companies.

Recommended company selection:

- Pick companies from different business models, such as manufacturing, consumer, technology, finance, or platform business.
- Prefer companies with public filings and enough information for verification.
- Avoid choosing only similar companies unless the user specifically wants peer comparison.

Output format:

1. Test setup
2. One short analysis per company
3. Cross-company comparison table
4. Weaknesses found in the Skill
5. Proposed Skill improvements

## Valuation and Scenario Guidance

If reliable data is available, use simple valuation logic:

- Comparable multiples: P/E, P/S, EV/EBITDA, P/B, or industry-specific multiples
- DCF-style scenario: revenue growth, margin, reinvestment, discount rate, terminal value
- Scenario table: pessimistic, base, optimistic

For course assignments, a simple scenario table is often better than a fragile complex model.

Example scenario table:

| Scenario | Core assumption | Expected result | Main risk |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Pessimistic | Growth slows and margins compress | Valuation pressure | Demand weaker than expected |
| Base | Growth follows industry trend | Moderate upside/downside | Execution uncertainty |
| Optimistic | Growth accelerates and margins expand | Higher valuation | Assumptions may be too aggressive |

## Human-AI Collaboration Evidence

The final report should include a short process record:

- Initial user prompt
- AI's first structure proposal
- User feedback or correction
- Revised assumptions or sections
- Final changes before submission

This is important when the assignment requires proof of human-AI collaboration.
